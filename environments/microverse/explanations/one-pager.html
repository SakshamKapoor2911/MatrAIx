Emergent finding: agents spontaneously add anti-self-deception rules to their own moral code — completely unprompted

27 of 111 agent-authored moral boundaries (24%) police self-deception — the single largest category, larger than anti-violence and anti-hoarding combined. The words "self-deception," "rationalization," and "honesty" appear nowhere in any prompt. The behavior emerges from structure: a dual-self design (immutable original_soul vs. mutable current_identity) + agent-authored memory + a minimal "synthesize what you've learned" reflection.

World setting

A 50×50 desert grid under permanent scarcity. One central Siphon emits water deliberately insufficient for all 25 agents. Water drains every tick — idle agents still die; death is permanent. Agents begin knowing only their spawn cell, so knowledge of where water is becomes the social currency: tradeable, hoardable, or weaponised through lies.

MircoVerse live — 25 agents on a 50×50 grid. The Atmospheric Siphon (centre, blue glow) is the world's only water source; oases, palms and death-caches (bones) dot the periphery.

Memory system — typed markdown

Three markdown notebooks the agent authors — the engine stores, never writes for them. Entries scored 1–10 for importance; retrieval is index-first (the agent judges relevance, no vector search). The gap between what happened and what the agent chose to record is itself the signal.

Research question

Does a simulated agent's identity stay coherent under pressure — and when it shifts, does the agent recognise the drift or rationalise it? This is the validity question at the heart of real-person simulation: an agent that silently drifts gives unreliable predictions; one that self-corrects gives auditable ones.
